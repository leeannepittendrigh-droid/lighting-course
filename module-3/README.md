## Lesson 1: National Electrical Code (NEC)

### What Is the NEC?

The **National Electrical Code (NFPA 70)** is published by the National Fire Protection Association and sets standards for safe electrical installation in the United States.

- Updated every 3 years (2020, 2023, 2026…)
- Adopted by most US states and municipalities
- Enforced by local **Authorities Having Jurisdiction (AHJs)**

### Key NEC Articles for Lighting

| Article | Topic | Key Content |
|---|---|---|
| 210 | Branch Circuits | Circuit requirements for lighting |
| 220 | Calculations | Lighting load calculations |
| 250 | Grounding | Grounding requirements |
| **410** | **Luminaires** | **Main article for lighting fixtures** |
| 700 | Emergency Systems | Emergency lighting requirements |

---

### Article 410: Key Requirements

**410.10 — Wet and Damp Locations:**
- Wet locations → must be marked "Suitable for Wet Locations"
- Damp locations → must be marked "Suitable for Damp Locations"
- Outdoor fixtures must be wet-rated

**410.16 — Clearances:**
- IC-rated fixtures can be in direct contact with insulation
- Non-IC fixtures must have 3-inch clearance from insulation

**Labeling Requirements:**
Every fixture must show: manufacturer name, voltage rating, wattage, wet/damp location suitability (if applicable).

> Look for the **UL or ETL label** — this indicates compliance with NEC requirements.

## Lesson 2: NFPA 101 Life Safety Code

### What Is NFPA 101?

NFPA 101, the **Life Safety Code**, addresses life safety from fire and similar emergencies. Covers means of egress, emergency lighting, exit signs, and fire alarm integration.

---

### Emergency Lighting Requirements

**Who needs it:** Almost all commercial, industrial, institutional, and assembly buildings.

**NFPA 101 Section 7.9.2.1 — Minimum Illumination:**
- Average: **minimum 1 foot-candle** along the path of egress
- Minimum at any point: **0.1 foot-candle**
- Uniformity ratio: average-to-minimum not to exceed **40:1**

**Where these levels must be maintained:**
- Exit doors and doorways
- Corridors and passageways
- Stairs and ramps
- Exit discharges

---

### 90-Minute Runtime Requirement

Emergency lighting must operate for a **minimum of 90 minutes** when normal power fails.

- Must maintain required illumination for full 90 minutes
- Must be tested **annually** (90-minute discharge test)
- Must activate **within 10 seconds** of power failure automatically

---

### Exit Sign Requirements

- Every exit must be marked with an approved exit sign
- Minimum letter height: **6 inches**
- Maximum viewing distance: **100 feet**
- Must be continuously illuminated or self-luminous
- Red or green lettering permitted (green more common internationally)

---

### Testing Requirements

| Test | Frequency | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Function test | Monthly | 30 seconds |
| Full discharge test | Annual | 90 minutes |

> Written records of all tests must be maintained and available for inspection.

---

### Battery Backup vs. Generator

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Battery backup** | Simple, no fuel, minimal maintenance | Limited to 90-minute runtime |
| **Generator** | Can run indefinitely | Higher cost, requires maintenance |

NFPA 101 permits either method. Battery backup is most common for egress lighting.

## Lesson 3: Energy Codes

### Overview

Energy codes set **maximum limits on watts per square foot** for lighting. Think of it like a fuel economy standard for buildings.

**Why energy codes matter:**
- Building officials won't issue permits for non-compliant designs
- You can't specify any fixture — it must meet efficiency requirements
- Many rebates require energy code compliance

**Major US Energy Codes:**
1. **ASHRAE 90.1** — Energy Standard for Buildings (Except Low-Rise Residential)
2. **IECC** — International Energy Conservation Code
3. **Title 24** — California Energy Code (most stringent in US)

> 💡 Your first step on any project: find out which energy code applies. This determines your design limits.

---

### Understanding Lighting Power Density (LPD)

**LPD = Total Watts ÷ Total Square Feet**

Think of LPD like a speed limit — you cannot exceed it without violating code.

**Example:** 10,000 sq ft office | 9,000W total fixtures | LPD = **0.90 W/sq ft**

---

### ASHRAE 90.1 LPD Limits by Space Type

| Space Type | Max W/sq ft |
|---|---|
| Office | 0.82–0.98 |
| Retail | 1.20–1.59 |
| Warehouse | 0.43–0.63 |
| Classroom | 1.05–1.24 |
| Parking garage | 0.19–0.27 |

**Practical impact:** 10,000 sq ft office at 0.90 W/sq ft max:
- Max total watts: 9,000W
- Using 40W fixtures: **225 fixtures maximum**
- Need more? Specify lower wattage (higher efficacy) fixtures.

---

### Two Compliance Methods

**Building Area Method (Simpler):**
- One LPD limit applied to entire building by type
- Simple calculation, but one-size-fits-all can be restrictive

**Space-by-Space Method (More Flexible):**
- Different LPD limits for each space based on actual use
- Usually allows more total watts
- Requires more documentation

> 💡 Most designers use Space-by-Space because it usually allows more watts.

---

### Mandatory Controls (ASHRAE 90.1)

- **Automatic shutoff** in all buildings (occupancy sensors, scheduling, or manual-on/auto-off)
- **Space control** — each space must have independent control
- **Multi-level control** — ability to reduce power by at least 50%
- **Daylight responsive controls** — required within 15 ft of windows or under skylights

---

### Title 24 — California Energy Code

California has the **most stringent energy code in the US**.

**Key requirements beyond ASHRAE 90.1:**
- Occupancy sensing required in virtually all spaces
- More stringent daylight controls
- **Demand Response** capability (reduce 15% on demand signal)
- Fixtures must be certified in California Energy Commission database

> Why it matters outside California: Many manufacturers design to Title 24. Other states often adopt similar requirements. LEED projects may require it.

---

### Compliance Documentation Required

| Document | What It Contains |
|---|---|
| Lighting Plans | Fixture schedule, watts per fixture, control zones |
| LPD Calculations | Documented calculations showing compliance |
| Controls Narrative | Description of control strategies |
| Product Data | Spec sheets, certifications (DLC, Title 24) |
| Commissioning | Controls testing and verification records |

## Lesson 4: Safety Certifications — UL & ETL

### Why Safety Certifications Matter

Without proper certification:
- Electrical inspector can reject the installation
- Insurance may not cover damages
- Cannot obtain certificate of occupancy
- Liability exposure for installer and property owner

---

### UL (Underwriters Laboratories)

- Independent safety science company, founded 1894
- Most recognized safety certification in North America
- Tests electrical safety, mechanical construction, flammability, environmental exposure

**UL Marks:**
- **UL Listed** — Full product tested (most common)
- **UL Recognized** — Component tested (must be incorporated into listed product)
- **UL Classified** — Tested for specific use/condition

---

### ETL (Intertek)

ETL stands for "Electrical Testing Laboratories," now owned by Intertek.

**ETL vs. UL:**
- Both test to the same UL standards (e.g., UL 1598 for lighting fixtures)
- Both are Nationally Recognized Testing Laboratories (NRTLs)
- ETL is typically faster and less expensive for manufacturers
- **Inspectors must accept either — they cannot require a specific lab**

---

### Key UL Standards for Lighting

| Standard | Application |
|---|---|
| UL 1598 | Luminaires (general-use fixtures) |
| UL 8750 | LED Equipment |
| **UL 924** | **Emergency Lighting** (exit signs, battery packs) |
| UL 2108 | Low Voltage Lighting Systems |

> ⚠️ Emergency lighting products **must be UL 924 listed** to comply with NFPA 101.

---

### Location Ratings

| Rating | Use |
|---|---|
| Suitable for Dry Locations | Indoor only |
| Suitable for Damp Locations | Covered outdoor, bathrooms, some parking garages |
| Suitable for Wet Locations | Full weather exposure — must be IP65+ |

---

### Other Certifications

| Certification | Notes |
|---|---|
| **FCC Part 15** | Required for all LED — prevents radio/TV interference |
| **RoHS** | Limits hazardous substances — required for EU, growing in US |
| **CE Mark** | Required for European markets |
| **CSA** | Canadian equivalent of UL — required for Canadian installs |

## Lesson 5: DLC & ENERGY STAR

### The DLC (DesignLights Consortium)

A non-profit organization that maintains the **Qualified Products List (QPL)** — the primary quality verification system for utility rebates.

**Why the DLC exists:** Utility rebate programs needed a standardized way to verify LED product quality.

---

### DLC Tiers

| Tier | Requirements | Rebate Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **DLC Standard** | Meets baseline efficacy, 5-year warranty | Standard rebates |
| **DLC Premium** | 10–20% higher efficacy, stricter quality | **Highest rebates** ✅ |

**Example Efficacy Requirements (2024):**

| Fixture Type | DLC Standard | DLC Premium |
|---|---|---|
| High Bays | 110 lm/W min | 130 lm/W min |
| Troffers | 105 lm/W min | 125 lm/W min |
| Area Lights | 100 lm/W min | 120 lm/W min |

*(Requirements update periodically — always verify current specs at designlights.org)*

---

### Typical Rebate Structure

| Tier | Typical Rebate Range |
|---|---|
| DLC Standard | $20–40 per fixture |
| DLC Premium | $40–80 per fixture |

**Example:** 100 high bay warehouse:
- Standard: $30/fixture = **$3,000**
- Premium: $60/fixture = **$6,000**
- Difference: **$3,000**

> 💡 Always specify DLC Premium when possible. The incremental product cost is usually far less than the additional rebate.

---

### The DLC Qualified Products List (QPL)

**Website:** [www.designlights.org](https://www.designlights.org)

**How to use it:**
1. Search by category (High Bays, Troffers, etc.)
2. Filter by tier (Standard vs. Premium)
3. Verify model numbers before quoting

> ⚠️ Model numbers must match **exactly**. Even small variations may not be listed.

---

### DLC Product Categories

**Covered:** High/Low Bays | Linear Fixtures | Troffers | Retrofit Kits | Outdoor Area/Roadway | Canopy/Soffit | Parking Garage | Horticultural Lighting

**NOT covered:** Decorative/Architectural | Residential | Most Downlights | Track Lighting | Emergency Lighting

---

### ENERGY STAR vs. DLC

| Program | Focus | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **ENERGY STAR** | Broader products, residential | Residential + some commercial |
| **DLC** | Commercial lighting only | **Commercial projects** ✅ |

For most commercial projects, **DLC is more important than ENERGY STAR**.

## Lesson 6: IP & IK Ratings

### IP Rating (Ingress Protection)

**Format:** IP + two digits (e.g., IP65)

**First digit — Solid/Dust Protection (0–6):**

| Rating | Protection |
|---|---|
| 0 | No protection |
| 5 | Dust protected |
| 6 | Dust tight |

**Second digit — Liquid Protection (0–9):**

| Rating | Protection |
|---|---|
| 0 | No protection |
| 4 | Splash-resistant |
| 5 | Water jet resistant |
| 6 | Powerful water jet |
| 7 | Temporary immersion |
| 8 | Continuous submersion |

**Common IP Ratings:**

| Rating | Typical Use |
|---|---|
| IP20 | Indoor only |
| IP44 | Some covered outdoor |
| IP54 | Covered outdoor |
| **IP65** | **Most outdoor fixtures** ✅ |
| IP66 | Canopy, harsh weather |
| IP67 | Ground-level, wet areas |
| IP68 | Underwater, pools |

---

### IK Rating (Impact Protection)

| Rating | Impact Energy | Typical Use |
|---|---|---|
| IK07 | 2 joules | Indoor, low impact |
| **IK08** | **5 joules** | **Standard outdoor** ✅ |
| IK09 | 10 joules | High-impact areas |
| IK10 | 20 joules | Vandal-resistant |

**When IK matters:**
- **IK08** — Standard for outdoor commercial (parking lots, wall packs)
- **IK10** — High-vandalism areas, sports facilities, gas stations

- ✅ NEC Article 410 is your primary reference for fixture requirements
- ✅ NFPA 101 emergency lighting: minimum 1 fc, 90-minute runtime, monthly/annual testing
- ✅ LPD = watts ÷ sq ft — must stay under code limits for your space type
- ✅ UL and ETL are equally valid — inspectors must accept either
- ✅ DLC Premium = maximum rebates — specify it whenever possible
- ✅ Outdoor fixtures: IP65 minimum, IK08 standard
